FASHION STYLE GUIDES

MINIMALIST STYLE
Minimalism in fashion is not about owning less -- it is about wearing less at once. The goal is paring back to intentional, cohesive looks built from timeless staples. Everything in a minimalist wardrobe should work interchangeably. Key pieces: well-fitted trousers, crisp white shirts, simple knitwear, quality basics in neutral tones. The fit must be excellent -- minimalism has nowhere to hide poor tailoring. Accessories should be sparse and geometric. Stick to muted tones; when you do introduce a pop of color or a subtle print, make it the center of the outfit. An all-neutral palette -- black and white, navy and cream -- can have as striking an effect as bold colors when the proportions and textures are right.

SMART CASUAL
Smart casual bridges formal and relaxed. The core principle: combine one elevated piece with one casual piece. A blazer with dark jeans, tailored trousers with a clean white tee, quality leather shoes with a simple knit. Never pair two overtly casual pieces (graphic tee with joggers) or two very formal pieces in a smart casual context -- one of each maintains the balance. A well-tailored blazer instantly elevates almost any outfit. Fit is critical: ill-fitting clothing undermines the entire aesthetic regardless of price.

QUIET LUXURY AND OLD MONEY AESTHETIC
Quiet luxury -- also called stealth wealth or old money aesthetic -- is about understated quality rather than visible logos or trendy pieces. The palette is neutral and muted: camel, cream, ivory, white, navy, grey, forest green. Fabrics matter more than cuts -- cashmere, silk, fine wool, linen, and cotton over polyester or synthetic blends. Key pieces: tailored trousers, a white poplin shirt, slip dresses, knit dresses, leather loafers, ankle boots in soft leather, minimal gold jewellery, a structured neutral tote. Accessories should be subtle: gold hoops, a signet ring, a silk scarf. Prioritize tonal dressing -- layering different shades of the same neutral creates visual cohesion. Impeccable tailoring and fit are the foundation.

STREETWEAR
Streetwear is defined by oversized silhouettes, graphic elements, athletic influences, and premium sneakers. The key to looking intentional rather than sloppy is proportion contrast: pair an oversized top with a fitted bottom, or a fitted top with wide-leg trousers -- never two oversized pieces together. Clean footwear is essential; well-maintained sneakers anchor the look. Keep jewelry minimal. Color-blocking, graphic tees, hoodies, and cargo pants are core elements, but the overall silhouette should still read as considered.

CASUAL CHIC
Casual chic lives in the space between streetwear and formal -- polished but not overdressed, comfortable but not sloppy. Core wardrobe pieces: a tailored blazer, a simple white tee, tailored trousers, dark wash jeans, classic loafers or white leather sneakers, a trench coat. Natural fibers -- cotton, linen, wool, silk -- add inherent quality and drape well. Replace leggings with tailored trousers, swap graphic tees for crisp button-downs, layer thoughtfully, and keep accessories classic. Quality over quantity: fewer, better pieces always outperform a wardrobe of trend-driven fast fashion.

PROPORTION AND SILHOUETTE
Proportion is the single most important element in outfit building. Balance volume: an oversized top demands a slim or fitted bottom; wide-leg trousers work best with a fitted or tucked-in top. Avoid wearing two oversized or two very fitted pieces together -- the outfit loses shape. The silhouette should always be readable. When mixing formal and casual pieces, one structured piece combined with one relaxed piece creates the most wearable results.

SHOES SET THE REGISTER
Shoes establish the formality of the entire outfit. Clean white sneakers make almost anything look casual and contemporary. Loafers dress up casual pieces. Heels or smart flats elevate denim to dinner-appropriate. Always match shoe formality to the most formal piece in your outfit, not the least formal. A tailored outfit with worn or inappropriate shoes undermines every other element.

TEXTURE AND FABRIC MIXING
Mixing textures adds depth and visual interest to simple outfits. Pair silk with wool, matte with shine, smooth with knit, stiff with soft. This is especially important in monochromatic outfits where color contrast is intentionally minimal. In autumn and winter, mixing corduroy, denim, knitwear, and leather creates layered richness. In spring and summer, pairing linen with cotton or silk keeps looks light while adding dimension.
